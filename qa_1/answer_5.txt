Question (from finals page — Q5):
Explain cosine similarity in plain language. If two document vectors point in almost the same direction, what does that tell us about the documents they represent? You don't need to write the formula — a geometric or intuitive explanation is enough.

Expected: an intuitive geometric explanation and what near-equal direction implies about document similarity; optional note about when Euclidean distance may be a poor choice.

Answer:

Cosine similarity measures how aligned two vectors are by looking at the angle between them. If two document vectors point in almost the same direction, their cosine similarity is high — this means the documents share similar semantic content or topics, even if they use different words.

Why use cosine not Euclidean (brief):
Cosine focuses on direction and ignores vector length, so it captures semantic orientation even when document lengths or embedding magnitudes differ. Euclidean distance can be dominated by magnitude differences (long vs short documents) and is less robust in high-dimensional embedding spaces.

One-sentence summary:
Cosine similarity tells you whether vectors point the same way (semantic similarity); near-equal direction means the documents are semantically close.
