Question (from finals page — Q4):
What is a sentence embedding? How is it fundamentally different from one-hot encoding? Give one advantage embeddings have that one-hot vectors don't. Think about: what can you measure between two embeddings that you can't measure between two one-hot vectors?

Expected: short explanation of embeddings vs one-hot and one concrete advantage (e.g., semantic similarity via distance/cosine).

Answer:

A sentence embedding is a fixed-length numeric vector that represents the semantic meaning of a sentence in a continuous vector space. Unlike a one-hot vector (which is sparse and only indicates presence/identity of exact tokens), embeddings capture semantic relationships: similar sentences map to nearby vectors.

Concrete advantage:
Embeddings let you measure semantic similarity (e.g., using cosine similarity or Euclidean distance) so you can find semantically related sentences even when they share no exact words — something one-hot vectors cannot do because one-hot representations are orthogonal for different tokens.

One-sentence summary:
Sentence embeddings map text to a continuous space where distance reflects semantic similarity; one-hot vectors cannot represent meaning or similarity beyond exact token identity.
